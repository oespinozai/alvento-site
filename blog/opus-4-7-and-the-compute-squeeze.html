Opus 4.7 came out worse than 4.6, and the reasons matter

By Oscar Espinoza, Alvento - 22 April 2026 - 5 min read

Anthropic shipped Claude Opus 4.7 on 16 April. Within a day, side-by-side regressions were doing the rounds. On the MRCR long-context benchmark, 4.6 scored 78.3%. 4.7 scored 32.2%. The new tokenizer produces roughly 35% more tokens on the same input, so your bills go up even though the sticker price didn't. Reliability complaints came in from day one.

Four days later, Anthropic announced up to $25 billion in new investment from Amazon, a $100 billion cloud commitment to AWS, and access to 5 gigawatts of Trainium capacity. The announcement describes the infrastructure pressure from enterprise and consumer demand as "inevitable strain."

In parallel, Anthropic is running a withheld frontier model called Mythos. It scored 93.9% on SWE-bench and 97.6% on USAMO, and is available to around 40 organisations through Project Glasswing, Anthropic's cybersecurity initiative. That looks like where the best compute is going. Opus 4.7 looks like what shipped on what was left.

What to do if you run Claude in production

Higher version numbers no longer reliably mean better

Higher numbers used to mean better across the board. That's stopped being reliable. Releases are shaped by infrastructure trade-offs, research priorities, and commercial deals you can't see from outside. The same lab can ship a frontier system to 40 security partners and a degraded public version to everyone else in the same month.

Treat model selection as procurement

Run 4.6 and 4.7 on your own prompts before you switch anything. Pin your model version in production so you're not auto-upgraded into a regression. If you haven't got automated regression testing for model behaviour yet, this is the quarter to build one.

Lab decisions are vendor risk now

Compute allocation, partnership deals, and research priorities at Anthropic, OpenAI and Google feed directly into the tools your business relies on. They're not going to get more stable. Treat the labs the way you'd treat any other critical vendor: with contracts, fallbacks, and a view of what breaks if the supplier changes direction.

AI compute isn't abundant. It's contested, expensive, and allocated in ways that aren't public. If you treat each new release as a free upgrade, you will get caught out at some point. A couple of hours of checking this week is cheap insurance against that.
